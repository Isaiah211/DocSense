In 1948 he published a little-known essay called Intelligent Machinery in which he outlined two forms of computing he felt could ultimately lead to machines exhibiting intelligent behavior. The first was a variant of his highly influential “universal Turing machine”, destined to become the foundation for hardware design in all modern digital computers. The second was an idea he called an “unorganized machine”, a type of computer that would use a network of “artificial neurons” to accept inputs and translate them into predicted outputs.
